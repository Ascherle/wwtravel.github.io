Russia, a country that stretches over a vast expanse of eastern Europe and northern Asia. Once the preeminent republic of the Union of Soviet Socialist Republics (U.S.S.R.; commonly known as the Soviet Union), Russia became an independent country after the dissolution of the Soviet Union in December 1991.

The inhabitants of Russia are quite diverse. Most are ethnic Russians, but there also are more than 120 other ethnic groups present, speaking many languages and following disparate religious and cultural traditions. Most of the Russian population is concentrated in the European portion of the country, especially in the fertile region surrounding Moscow, the capital. Moscow and St. Petersburg (formerly Leningrad) are the two most important cultural and financial centres in Russia and are among the most picturesque cities in the world. Russians are also populous in Asia, however; beginning in the 17th century, and particularly pronounced throughout much of the 20th century, a steady flow of ethnic Russians and Russian-speaking people moved eastward into Siberia, where cities such as Vladivostok and Irkutsk now flourish.

Walking around Moscow

On this trip my brother and I were together in Russia for the first time since I was very little! Because Russia has a military draft system for men under 27, he was finally able to come back to Russia in 2016. We took advantage of our time well and filled every day with new adventure.

My brother and I

Moscow is huge, and the roads are very wide to let many lanes of traffic speed by. It takes so long to get from one place to another without using the metro but we also wanted to just explore the streets on foot and people watch for a while. In the photo my brother and I are standing on a bridge overlooking the Moskva River, with the statue of Peter the Great‚Äôs ship visible in the background. We had street food for lunch, hot piroshky, and then went to the Pushkin Museum, the largest museum of European art in Moscow.

A very Russian Cat

Although he has no relation to the museum, it was renamed the Pushkin Museum after our beloved writer and poet Alexander Pushkin, 100 years after his death. During our visit, the museum had a modern photography exhibition with various photos of Russian life and scenery. One that particularly caught my eye was a very Russian themed photo of a cat on a table next to blini (Russian crepes), vodka, and caviar. The caption says ‚Äúwho doesn't work, eats‚Äù, which is a joke on the common Russian phrase "who doesn't work, doesn't get to eat", because we feed house cats no matter how lazy they are!

At the Dacha

Dacha in Russian is a word we use for a summer home, where we grow vegetables and relax away from city life. Our family dacha was built by my Grandfather who unfortunately died right before I was born. Because it has been in the family for over 40 years, one can sense it is a place full of memories. Sadly the building itself was never fully completed so the interior has always looked a bit unfinished. Over the years, so many things were stored away, especially because of Soviet era poverty. Not knowing what will be available the next week created a mindset for my Grandma and Grandpa to hold on to everything. Because of this, my Brother and I had to help my Aunt and Uncle clean out the shed and various rooms on the dacha.

Walking around the Dacha

So many old books and papers had to be burned because Russia doesn't have a good recycling system yet. It wasn‚Äôt all about work though, I got to walk around the area and enjoy the summer sun. Although it is a place we will never forget and will love forever, we were happy to get it cleaned out to be sold. We have a new plot of land granted by my Aunt's work, which is ready for new beginnings. I made sure to get plenty of photos because I knew I'd probably never see it again.


15/8/16
Karelia

Karelia is a subarctic region far up north divided between Finland and Russia. My Aunt, Brother, and I took a long bus excursion from Dmitrov, where we live, all the way to the Finnish border to see this beautiful region. We saw a beautiful Orthodox monastery on Valaam Island, located on Lake Ladoga. The monastery is believed to have been founded at some point toward the end of the 14th century.

Valaam Monastery

The church itself is a beautiful bright white structure with light blue-green onion domes and accents of gold. All about the territory were friendly cats who are fed by the nuns and priests. We got to hear the priests sing Byzantine chants and watch a student of iconography at work.

Crossing the White Sea


We then took a boat across the White Sea to the Solovetsky Islands where we saw the old Solovki Prison Camp. Many of the Bolshevik opponents were sent to this gulag because of its remote location. Before becoming a work camp during the soviet era, it was also a monastery, and has since resumed this status. The sea was very stormy on our way back and my coat soaked through, but it was certainly worth it to see the beautiful nature and architecture of this region.
